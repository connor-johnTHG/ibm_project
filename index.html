Meet IBM

International Business Machines — the company that basically invented the modern office. Founded way back in 1911, IBM spent decades building the machines that ran the world's biggest businesses, governments, and research labs. From punch card tabulators to room-sized mainframes, if something important was being calculated in the 20th century, chances are an IBM machine was doing it. By the time the 1980s rolled around, IBM had already put a man on the moon, helped run the US census, and built the systems that kept the world's banks from going haywire — not bad for a company that started out making scales and clocks!


Then in 1981, IBM did something that changed everything — they released the IBM Personal Computer, the PC 5150, and suddenly computing wasn't just for corporations and scientists anymore. It was for everyone. Your dad, your neighbor, your local travel agent. IBM didn't just make a computer — they accidentally invented the blueprint that every Windows machine since has been built on. Love your laptop? Thank IBM. Use a spreadsheet at work? Thank IBM. The whole idea of a personal computer sitting on a desk being useful to a regular human being? That was IBM's gift to the world — and they gave it to us wearing a suit and a smile."
